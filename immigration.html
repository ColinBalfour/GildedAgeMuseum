Foreigners Seek Refuge In America

It was a rough time period for the much of the world. Many people, especially from Eastern Europe and Asia, sought refuge in America. Word spread about the possibility to create a better life for you or your family in America. Immigrants quickly poured through checkpoints like Ellis island: most taken, some rejected. They were happy to have the chance to make a living - often immigrating with little education or skill. Anti-immigrant sentiment quickly rose, however, as “native” Americans blamed the new immigrants for their issues. The new immigrants were treated poorly - by people and law - often forcing them to return to the country they emigrated from.

The Unstoppable Boy

Benjamin Axelrod was a 12 year old immigrant from Russian Podolia (modern day Ukraine). He left home for America, likely due to being a burden on his family (better off on his own), which was unfortunately common at the time. After traveling to the US, he was rejected admission. Despite this, Axelrod hopped on an immigrant ship time and time again, sneaking back into America for yet another try. By 1907, he had been to Ellis Island (the checkpoint for immigrants) 7 times over the span of only 2 years. He was nicknamed “The Unstoppable Boy” for this reason.

The Chinese Exclusion Act

In 1882, the Chinese Exclusion Act was passed. It prevented Chinese immigrants in the US from being citizens, from sending for their families, and prohibited new immigrants arriving from China. Soon after, Frank Leslie drew a cartoon (image above) capturing the immorality with the act. It depicts a Chinese man sitting outside the gates of the US. the gate into America will accept all kinds of others - even those often portrayed as enemies of the US - but not the Chinese. The man sits outside the gates where the Chinse, peace, order, and industry are rejected. To top it off, Leslie leaves the message, “We have to draw the line somewhere, you know?”
