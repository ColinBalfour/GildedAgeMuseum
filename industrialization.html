A New Way of Life

Fully forming in the late 1800s, there was a push for industrialization. The economy of the US was rapidly transforming from agriculture to manufacturing. This spurred a revolution of factory jobs with assembly lines for mass production. Along with this came an influx of people who were happy to work these factory jobs. Workers of the time were often exploited by the factory owners as they squeezed out every last bit of profit.

Charlie Chaplin's Modern Times

Charlie Chaplin’s Modern Times in 1936 (image above) was a both hilarious and profound look into some of the issues with the new work lifestyle prevalent throughout the Gilded Age. Charlie Chaplin was a famed comedian of the time, and wanted to shed light on some of the issues with the new age. He depicted a worker who could not control his own impulses: unable to leave the assembly line without continuing the same motions. Chaplin demonstrates how workers had mandated and rote jobs to such an extreme that it was all they knew how to do.

Scientific Management & Taylorism

In 1911, Frederick Taylor wrote his influential “Scientific Management”. In this, he described a new management system, based on the scientific method. Workers would be tested with different methods to find which is the optimal performance. Optimal motions, tools, carrying weight; anything he could think of was tested and optimized. Managers were also supposed to have the same jobs as workers to ensure the process works.

Climate Effects

In the Bulletin of the Essex Institute Vol. 18 (published in Salem, MA - 1886), a section “The Climatology of the United States” was written from a lecture given by Frank R. Kimball. He makes some very interesting points that seem way ahead of the time (from my understanding) - synonymous to the worldview today. Many odd weather patterns were starting to occur throughout the 1880s: “We have had cold rainy Aprils more like November, with a few instances of unseasonable heat” (Bulletin 48). Not only were there strange occurrences in weather, but also “The prevalence of droughts and floods”. “In a deforested region, the effect…[is produced] by the water reaching the streams more rapidly” (Bulletin 49).

The Lasting Change on Civilization

While this all seems great on the surface, in practice it left workers vulnerable to be exploited by management. There were little precautions for worker rights and health. Workers could no longer find pleasure in their jobs, because a repetitive and optimized routine leaves no room for expression. Working conditions continued to plummet throughout the Gilded Age, depriving people of their needs with no intention to change.

It is clear that there were prevalent climate issues in the late 1800s, exacerbated by industrialization and the pollution and environmental damage it caused. As stated by Kimball, “The climate of a country has a greater influence upon the health and prosperity of the people than is generally realized” (Bulletin 15). The deforestation was at such an extreme due to the increasing industrialization that Vermont was stripped of almost all harvestable trees. This caused many problems, including droughts and floods (as mentioned by Kimball) heavily impacting the lives of citizens. Not much was done about the increasing problems with the climate, which as we know today is a global issue.

The impact of the new factories was not unknown at the time, either. In 1896 Svante Arrhenius wrote an article on the influence of CO2 emissions. Arrhenius’ work explained that halving the CO2 could drop the Earth’s temperature by 4-5°C. Despite this, nothing was done. The issues then are even more of a concern today due to its effect on global warming.
